U.S. Department of Education

Responsive Resdesign Case Study

Responsive redesign of the U.S. Department of Education with mobile and web hi-fi prototype. UX research, heuristic analysis, information architecture redesign, style guide, and hi-fi prototypes.

Project Overview

My UX Bootcamp gave us the challenge of redesigning a government agency website. We selected the U.S. Department of Education due to its visually disorganized appearance and lack of clear, intuitive navigation. The website has an overwhelming amount of important information geared towards students, educators, and researchers in the education sector. Redesigning to clarify, visual organize, and restructure took many steps. Read on to see how we did it all.

Project Scope

Research & Definition: Interviews & User Testing, Heurisitic Evaluation, Personas
Design & Development: Card Sorting, Sitemapping, Wireframes (low to high fidelity), Prototyping, User Testing
Visual Design: Mood boards, Color Accessibility testing, Style Guide development

Project Information

My Roles: UX Researcher and UI Designer
Collaborator: Jenna-Lee Stevens, research phase
Timeline: 5 Weeks

User Tests
We conducted 5 user tests on the existing website revealed general confusion and lack of clarity. Users felt that the website had everything they needed, but they struggled to find it easily and quickly.

User Personas

The variety and breadth of information on the website led us to developing two user personas that each encompass different user goals and frustrations. We referred to them throughout the entire redesign process to frame the problems and potential solutions.

A heuristic evaluation and homepage redlining and annotations were done on the website to gain an understanding of the problems we faced with the redesign. The website lacked intuitive navigation and information architecture.

New Sitemap

Information Archictecture

A card sorting expertise was conducted to redesign the current information architecture. Using Miro made it easy to visualize the current information architecture and allowed for quick card sorting. It took a few iterations to really get it down and later during user testing, I had to revisit the page labels on the navigation and adjust a few for clarity based on user testing feedback. A final sitemap was created and used as a guide for wireframes and prototyping.

Design

Wireframes

At the beginning of my design process I created wireframes in Adobe XD for mobile and web versions, starting with the navigation design first. Once I had tested and iterated the main and footer navigations, I continued to wireframe out the homepage.

Starting with the navigation helped me focus on creating a clear and intuitive layout that made it easy for users to find the specific information they are looking for quickly.

I tested the homepage layout with users to find problems and areas of confusion, as well as what was working right and users enjoyed about the initial design.

Conclusion & Future Opportunities

The major challenge I faced in this project was organizing and prioritizing the massive wealth of information of ed.gov. With patience, I was able to pick apart and reassemble each main page, using my initial research and personas to guide the restructuring of information, finally creating a cohesive and understandable navigation system and homepage layout.

Next steps include an additional hi-fi iteration and a tablet version.
